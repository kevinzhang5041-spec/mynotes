Flux in thermodynamics is defined as $$\text{molecular flux} = \frac{\text{number of molecules}}{\text{area} \times \text{time}}.$$
Recall that between an angle $\theta$ and $\theta+d\theta$, the number of molecules per unit volume travelling at speeds between $v$ and $v+dv$ is $$n f(v) dv \frac{1}{2} \sin \theta d\theta,$$where $f(v)$ is the speed distribution function satisfying $$f(v) \propto v^2 dv \cdot e^{-mv^2/2k_B T}.$$Thus, the number of molecules hitting a unit area of wall in unit time per unit volume, travelling at speed $v$ and angle $\theta$ (for conciseness) is $$(v \cos \theta)(n f(v) dv \frac{1}{2} \sin \theta d \theta).$$ The flux is then $$\begin{align*} \Phi &= \int_{0}^{\infty} \int_{0}^{\pi/2} (v \cos \theta) (n f(v) dv \frac{1}{2} \sin \theta d\theta)\\ &= \frac{n}{2}\int_0^\infty v f(v) dv \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin \theta \cos \theta d\theta \\ &= \frac{1}{4} n \langle v \rangle.\end{align*}$$Substituting the expression for $\langle v \rangle$ yields $$\Phi \propto \frac{p}{\sqrt {mT}}.$$


**NOTE:** This last proportionality for flux is useful when dealing with effusion, as a system in the effusion regime (when the diameter of the hole $D \ll \lambda$, the mean free path of the gas) is in equilibrium when the efflux equals the influx of gas. So, $$\frac{p_1}{\sqrt{T_1}} = \frac{p_2}{\sqrt{T_2}}.$$The effusion regime is when $D \ll \lambda$ because this is when a particle can escape through the hole (traveling distances on the order of $D$) without colliding with any others, so no pressure gradient develops (diffusion).
