Consider a prism with apex angle $\alpha$ and angle conventions as in this figure.
![[Pasted image 20240818002340.png]]
By Snell's Law and geometry, we obtain $$\begin{align*} \sin \theta_1 &= n \sin \theta_2 \\ \theta_2 + \theta_3 &= \alpha \\ \sin \theta_4 &= n \sin \theta_3. \end{align*}$$The angle of deviation $\delta = (\theta_1 - \theta_2) + (\theta_4 - \theta_3) = \theta_1 + \theta_4 - \alpha$. At minimum deviation, $\frac{d \delta}{d \theta_1} = 0$, so $d \theta_1 = - d \theta_4$.
Differentiating the two Snell's Laws equations (!), $$\begin{align*} \cos \theta_1 \, d\theta_1 &= n \cos \theta_2 \, d\theta_2 \\ \cos \theta_4 \, d\theta_4 &= n \cos \theta_3 \, d\theta_3. \end{align*} $$Dividing and substituting $d\theta_2 = -d\theta_3$, we see $$\begin{align*} \frac{\cos \theta_2}{\cos \theta_3} = \frac{\cos \theta_1}{\cos \theta_4} &= \frac{\sqrt{1 - \frac{\sin^2 \theta_1}{n^2}}}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{\sin^2 \theta_4}{n^2}}} \\ \implies \frac{\sqrt{1 - \cos^2 \theta_1}}{\sqrt{1 - \cos^2 \theta_4}} &= \frac{\sqrt{n^2 - \cos^2 \theta_1}}{\sqrt{n^2 - \cos^2 \theta_4}}.\end{align*}$$This can only occur when $\boxed{\theta_1 = \theta_4}$, or when $\theta_2 = \theta_3 = \frac{\alpha}{2}$. Then, $\theta_1 = \sin^{-1} (n \sin \frac{\alpha}{2})$ and the minimum deviation angle is $$\boxed{\delta_{\min} = 2 \sin^{-1} \left(n \sin \frac{\alpha}{2} \right) - \alpha}.$$
