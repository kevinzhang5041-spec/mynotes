Work is change in energy, and is calculated by 
$$
W = \int_{x_1}^{x_2}F(x')\,dx'.
$$
For potential energy
$$
U(x) = - \int_{x_0}^{x}F(x')\, dx'.
$$
or 
$$
F(x) = -\frac{dU(x)}{dx}.
$$
For a system, the work-energy theorem is 
$$
W_{external} = \Delta K + \Delta U + \Delta K_{internal}.
$$
An equilibrium point is defined as 
$$
U'(x_0) = 0.
$$
Using this and taking the [[Math/Taylor Series]] around the equilibrium point, we can deduce for small oscillations the system follows a [[Simple Harmonic Motion|Hooke's law type force]] with an angular frequency of 
$$
\omega = \sqrt{\frac{U''(x_0)}{m}}.
$$
This result holds for any function $U(x)$ an at minimum (except at $U''(x_0) = 0$), as the function will look like a parabola at that point.

Do not use an energy based approach when dealing with [[Inherent Heat Loss|inherently inelastic processes]].

See [[Energy in 3D]].

Energy can be decomposed of 
$$
\sum_i \frac{1}{2}m_iv_i^2 = \frac{1}{2}Mv_{CM}^2 + \sum_i \frac{1}{2}m_i(v_i-v_{cm})^2,
$$
where $M$ is the total mass.
