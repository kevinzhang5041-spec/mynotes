The conserved quantities of momentum and energy are 
$$
\textbf{p} = \gamma m\textbf{v}
$$
$$
E = \gamma mc^2
$$
and can be related as 
$$E^2 = p^2c^2 +m^2c^4$$
Transforming to a frame with velocity $v$ is
$$E' = \gamma(E-vp_x)$$
$$p_x' = \gamma(p_x-vE/c^2)$$
which implies that
$$
E^2-p^2=E'^2-p'^2
$$

The massless photons can be described as
$$E = pc$$
One other way to express momentum is
$$
p = \sqrt{E^2-m^2}
$$
or that 
$$
p \cdot p = (E^2-|\textbf{p}|^2).
$$
A force $F$ in a singular direction can be expressed as
$$
F=\frac{dE}{dx} = \gamma^3ma.
$$
And a four-force can be expressed as 
$$
f^{\mu}= (\gamma\frac{dE}{dt}, \gamma \textbf{F})
$$
which can then imply
$$
\Delta m = \int f^\mu dx_\mu.
$$
Assuming that $v_y$ is initially zero for a particle under a two dimensional force,
$$
\textbf{F} \equiv(\gamma^3a_x,\gamma a_y).
$$
and when transformed the instantaneous rest frame of the particle is
$$
(F'_x,F'_y)=m(\gamma^3a_x,\gamma^2a_y)
$$
which implies
$$
F_x=F'_x,
$$
$$F_y=\frac{F'_y}{\gamma}.$$
a useful fact is that
$$E = \frac{p}{v}
