Consider a situation where electromagnetic energy propagates in the +x direction, with $\bf{E}$ pointing in the +y direction and $\bf{B}$ pointing in the +z direction.

Applying Faraday's Law on an elemental loop between $x, x+dx$ and $y, y+dy$, we see $$\begin{align*} \oint \textbf{E} \cdot \textbf{dl} &= -\frac{d}{dt} \int \bf{B} \cdot \bf{dA} \\ E_y (x) \, dx - E_y (x+dx) \, dx &= -\frac{d}{dt} (- B_z \, dx \, dy) \\ \frac{\partial E_y}{\partial x} &= -\frac{\partial B_z}{\partial t}.   \end{align*}$$Applying Ampere-Maxwell's Law on an elemental loop between $x, x+dx$ and $z, z+dz$, we see $$\begin{align*} \oint \textbf{B} \cdot \textbf{dl} &= \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{d}{dt} \int \bf{E} \cdot dA \\ B_z (x) \, - B_z(x+dx) \, dz &= \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{d}{dt} (E_y \, dx \, dz) \\ \frac{\partial B_z}{\partial x} &= -\mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial E_y}{\partial t}. \end{align*}$$Differentiating both sides yields $\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \, \frac{\partial B_z}{\partial x} = - \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 E_y}{\partial t^2}$, which, by Clairaut's Theorem, is equivalent to $\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \frac{\partial B_z}{\partial t}$. Therefore, $$\frac{\partial^2 E_y}{\partial x^2} = \frac{1}{c^2} \frac{\partial^2 E_y}{\partial t^2} \hspace{1cm} \text{ and } \hspace{1cm} \frac{\partial^2 B_z}{\partial x^2} = \frac{1}{c^2} \frac{\partial^2 B_z}{\partial t^2}$$are the wave equations electric field and magnetic field, respectively, where $c = \frac{1}{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}$ is the speed of the EM wave (which is also the speed of light!).
