The electric field $E(x,y,z)$ is the electric force per unit charge at $(x,y,x)$.
To find the field of a point charge $q$, Considering a test charges $q_0$, and from [[Coulomb's Law]],
$$
q_0E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_0q}{d^2}
\implies E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{d^2}
$$
To find the field at a point from a charge arrangement with more than one point, [[Superimposition|superimpose]] the fields together.

For $N$ discrete charges:
$$
E(x,y,z) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\sum_{j=i}^{N} \frac{q_j\hat{r}_{0j}}{r^2_{0j}}
$$
For a distribution:
$$
E(x,y,z) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\frac{\rho(x', y', z')\hat{r}dx'dy'dz'}{r^2}
$$

Electric field lines can be drawn using field lines, which shows how the field "flows" in space.![[Field Lines]]
Field lines are especially useful when analyzing [[Electric Potential|electric potentials]] and [[Conductors|conductors]].
